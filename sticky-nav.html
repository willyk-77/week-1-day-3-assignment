This Is Kenya — Nine Places Every Soul Should See

A Journey Through Kenya's Greatest Wonders

Masai Mara

The Maasai Mara is Kenya's most celebrated wildlife reserve, stretching across 1,500 square kilometres of endless golden savannah in the southwest. It is home to the greatest wildlife spectacle on earth — the Great Wildebeest Migration — where over two million wildebeest, zebras, and gazelles thunder across the plains between July and October. The Mara is also one of Africa's finest year-round safari destinations, with some of the densest populations of lions, leopards, cheetahs, and elephants on the continent.

Lake Nakuru

Lake Nakuru Lake Nakuru sits within a national park in Kenya's Rift Valley and is one of the most visually stunning wildlife destinations on the continent. The alkaline lake is famous for its extraordinary flamingo populations — at peak season, over a million birds coat the shoreline in a vivid pink blanket visible from miles away. Beyond the flamingos, the park is a critical sanctuary for both endangered black and white rhinos, and its dense acacia forests shelter lions, leopards, pythons, and over 450 bird species.

Amboseli National Park

Amboseli sits at the foot of Mount Kilimanjaro, Africa's highest peak, and offers some of the most iconic wildlife photography backdrops in the world. The park is famous for its large herds of free-roaming elephants, many with sweeping tusks, moving gracefully across dusty plains with the snow-capped mountain rising behind them. Fed by underground streams from Kilimanjaro, Amboseli also supports a rich ecosystem of hippos, buffaloes, over 600 bird species, and the semi-nomadic Maasai people who have coexisted with wildlife here for centuries.

Mount Kenya

Mount Kenya is Africa's second highest mountain and a place of immense spiritual significance to the Kikuyu people, who regard its peaks as the throne of their creator god Ngai. Rising to 5,199 metres, the mountain is capped with dramatic glaciers, jagged rock spires, and equatorial snowfields that have inspired climbers and adventurers for generations. The lower slopes are blanketed in ancient montane forests full of elephants, buffaloes, and leopards, while the upper zones transition into otherworldly moorlands of giant lobelias and groundsels found nowhere else on earth.

Tsavo National Park

Tsavo National Park Tsavo is Kenya's largest national park, covering nearly 22,000 square kilometres of raw, untamed wilderness split into Tsavo East and Tsavo West. The park is legendary for its red elephants — giants that roll in the iron-rich red soil until their skin glows a deep terracotta — and for its man-eating lions that terrorised railway workers during the building of the Uganda Railway in the 1890s. Tsavo's dramatic landscape includes the Yatta Plateau, one of the world's longest lava flows, the crystal-clear Mzima Springs where hippos glide through underwater, and sweeping open plains stretching endlessly to the horizon.

Hell's Gate National Park

Hell's Gate National Park is one of Kenya's most dramatically beautiful and unique destinations, carved out of the Great Rift Valley and shaped by millions of years of volcanic and geothermal activity. Towering red and black gorges, roaring geysers, bubbling hot springs, and volcanic rock towers create a landscape so striking it reportedly inspired the scenery in Disney's The Lion King. Unlike most Kenyan parks, Hell's Gate allows visitors to explore entirely on foot or bicycle, cycling freely past grazing zebras, buffaloes, and gazelles before descending into the spectacular Fischer's Tower gorge for a guided hike through its steaming, cathedral-like walls.

Diani Beach

Diani Beach stretches for over 17 kilometres along Kenya's southern coastline, consistently ranked among the finest beaches in Africa and the world. Its powdery white coral sand, fringed by ancient baobab trees and swaying coconut palms, meets a warm turquoise Indian Ocean protected by a coral reef that keeps the waters calm and perfect for swimming. Beyond the beach, Diani offers world-class diving and snorkelling on pristine coral gardens, kite surfing on open waters, and the Shimba Hills forest just inland, home to rare sable antelopes and endangered elephants.

Lamu Old Town

Lamu Old Town is Kenya's oldest continuously inhabited town and one of the best-preserved examples of Swahili architecture and culture in East Africa, earning its place on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 2001. The town's narrow winding streets, too tight for cars, are navigated by donkeys and wooden hand carts, transporting visitors back through centuries of Swahili, Arab, Persian, and Portuguese history layered into every carved doorway and coral stone building. Lamu's culture remains deeply rooted in its Islamic heritage, expressed through its mosques, the annual Lamu Cultural Festival, and the graceful wooden dhows that have sailed these waters for over 700 years.

Nairobi National park

Nairobi National Park is one of the world's most extraordinary wildlife paradoxes — a fully wild game reserve sitting just seven kilometres from the centre of a bustling metropolis of five million people. Within its 117 square kilometres, lions sleep under acacia trees, rhinos graze on open plains, and giraffes stride across grasslands, all with Nairobi's glittering skyline rising dramatically on the horizon behind them. The park is also Kenya's most important rhino sanctuary and home to over 400 bird species, making it a critical conservation area that doubles as the most accessible safari destination in Africa.
